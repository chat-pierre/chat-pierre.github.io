Incommeasurable Quantities

In the early times of geometry the system of rational numbers has shown its weakness. As early as 5-4 century B.C., it has been shown by the Greeks that there is incommeasurable lengths of segments for a given unit. (Two numbers are commeasurable if the ratio of them is a rational number, since they can be both represented as a integral multiples of a common unit. )

Nested Intervals

Sequence of Nested Intervals: A sequence of closed intervals \(I_n=[a_n,b_n]\) on the real line, with each \(I_{n-1}\) contained in \(I_n\) and the lengths of the intervals tend to zero.

Postulate of Nested Intervals: If \(I_1,I_2,I_3,\dots\) form a nest sequences of intervals with rational endpoints, there is a point \(x\) contained in all \(I_n\).

N.B.

  • Such point is unique. If there are two such points, the lengths of the intervals cannot be arbitrarily small.

  • The intervals has to be closed. Consider the nested intervals \(I_n=[0,\frac{1}{n}]\). Obviously only \(0\) is in all \(I_n\). If end points are excluded, then there is no point contained in all such intervals.


Neighbourhood

A neighbourhood of a real number \(x_0\) is an open interval containing it.

\(\varepsilon\)-neighbourhood: For any positive \(\varepsilon\), the \(\varepsilon\)-neighbourhood of \(x_0\) consists of the value \(x\) such that \(x_0-\varepsilon<x<x_0+\varepsilon\). (also \((x_0-\varepsilon,x_0+\varepsilon)\), \(\vert x-x_0\vert<\varepsilon\))


Inequalities

Some important expansions:

  • difference of powers
\[\begin{align} a^2-b^2 &= (a-b)(a+b)\\ \Rightarrow a^n-b^n &= (a-b)(a^{n-1}+a^{n-2}b+\cdots+b^{n-1}) \end{align}\]
  • binomial expansion:
\[(a+b)^n=\sum_{i=0}^{n}\begin{pmatrix}n\\k\end{pmatrix}a^{n-k}b^k\]

Where \(\begin{pmatrix}n\\k\end{pmatrix}= \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}\) Some important inequalities:

  • triangle inequality
\[\begin{align} \vert a+b\vert&\leq\vert a\vert +\vert b\vert\\ \vert \alpha-\beta\vert&\leq\vert \alpha-\gamma\vert +\vert \gamma-\beta\vert\\ \vert a_1+a_2+\cdots+a_n\vert&\leq\vert a_1\vert+\vert a_2\vert+\cdots+\vert a_n\vert \end{align}\]
  • Cauchy-Schwarz inequality
\[(a_1b_1+a_2b_2+\cdots+a_nb_n)^2\leq(a_1^2+a_2^2+\cdots+a_n^2)(b_1^2+b_2^2+\cdots+b_n^2)\]

Proof of Cauchy-Schwarz inequality:

Let

\[\begin{align} A &= a_1^2+a_2^2+\cdots+a_n^2,\\ B &= b_1^2+b_2^2+\cdots+b_n^2,\\ C &= a_1b_1+a_2b_2+\cdots+a_nb_n \end{align}\]

We know that \(Ax^2-2Cx+B=(a_1x+b_1)^2+(a_2x+b_2)^2+\cdots+(a_nx+b_n)^2\geq0\).

Hence the discriminator \(4(C^2-AB)\geq0\).
